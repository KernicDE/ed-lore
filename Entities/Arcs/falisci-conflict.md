# Falisci Conflict

## Summary
The Falisci Conflict was an early test of Federal resolve against frontier independence movements. In early 3301, democratic forces in the Falisci system attempted to break free from Federal control. The Federation responded with overwhelming military force, crushing the uprising within weeks. The conflict demonstrated that while the Federation tolerated political dissent within its framework, it would not accept secession—a precedent that would be tested again in systems like Lugh and Kappa Fornacis.

## Phases

### Phase 1: The Uprising (3301-02-27)
Democratic forces in Falisci, organised under the Falisci Democracy faction, launched an open bid for independence from Federal control. Citing the right to self-determination, they appealed to independent pilots and sympathetic factions for support. The movement gained initial traction, with reports of successful resistance against Federal-aligned local forces.

### Phase 2: Federal Invasion (3301-03-04)
The Federation deployed military forces to Falisci in overwhelming numbers. Federal Navy vessels engaged independence forces while propaganda outlets framed the intervention as restoring order. The phrase "Falisci in flames" described the devastation as Federal forces systematically dismantled resistance positions. Independent pilots were caught between supporting democratic ideals and facing the might of a superpower military.

### Phase 3: Democracy Denied (3301-03-27)
Federal forces secured control of the Falisci system. The Falisci Democracy faction was defeated and its leaders arrested or killed. The Federation imposed martial law and installed compliant local administration. The swift, brutal suppression sent a clear message to other frontier systems contemplating independence: the Federation would not permit secession, and the cost of resistance was total defeat.

## Modern Relevance
The Falisci Conflict established the Federal template for handling independence movements—overwhelming force, rapid resolution, and no negotiation. This approach would be repeated in later conflicts, most notably the Lugh crisis, where the Alliance declined to intervene and the Federal Navy again crushed secessionist forces. The conflict also highlighted the hypocrisy at the heart of Federal ideology: a government claiming to represent democracy would not permit democratically expressed desires for independence.
