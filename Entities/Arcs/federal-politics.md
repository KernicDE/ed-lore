## Description

The Federal Politics arc traces a decade of transformation in the Federation's governance, from the collapse of the Liberal Party under President Jasmina Halsey to the Republican dominance of Zachary Hudson and the eventual Liberal restoration under Felicia Winters. It reveals how corporate interests, military expansionism, and surveillance technology reshaped Federal democracy — and how deeply these changes have become embedded in the superpower's political structure.

## Narrative

The arc begins in early 3301 with the terminal crisis of the Halsey administration. Elected in 3300 on a platform of social reform, Halsey saw her presidency unravel through a series of self-inflicted wounds: an unpopular campaign against the narcotic Onionhead that included firebombing civilian farms, multiple tax hikes, and the disastrous War for Lugh. The war's lowest point came when Halsey ordered an attack on an unarmed refugee convoy, killing approximately 9,322 civilians. Her approval rating collapsed to 16%, the lowest ever recorded for a sitting Federal president.

On 26 May 3301, Halsey's presidential vessel Starship One disappeared during a goodwill tour. The subsequent Vote of No Confidence on 2 June installed Zachary Hudson as President. For years, the destruction of Starship One was treated as a tragic accident. In 3307, the truth emerged: Fleet Admiral Lucas Vincent and Core Dynamics CEO Jupiter Rochester had conspired to destroy the vessel and assassinate Halsey, intending to install Hudson as a puppet who would ensure lucrative military contracts for Core Dynamics. Vincent was sentenced to life imprisonment; Rochester was jailed after his breakaway Jupiter Division was defeated in battle.

The Hudson administration (3301–3310) remade the Federation in its image. Military spending increased dramatically. The Proactive Detection Bureau (PDB) was established to monitor all private communications across Federal space, justified by the Thargoid threat but widely used for political surveillance. Corporate tax rebates and deregulation enriched Hudson's allies while cutting social programmes. The Close Quarters Combat championship was promoted as a naval recruitment tool. Hudson survived a mandatory Vote of No Confidence in 3305 by a narrow margin, demonstrating both his resilience and his vulnerability.

The arc's final phase began with the 3309 election. Hudson's chosen successor, Jerome Archer, proved unpopular — a weakness Hudson attempted to compensate for with last-minute legislation including media regulation, a political think tank, and the merger of the PDB into Federal Navy Intelligence. Felicia Winters, who had served as Halsey's Secretary of State and become Shadow President after Hudson's rise, campaigned on reversing these measures. She won, and took office on 31 October 3310, promising to dismantle the PDB, cut military spending, and restore civil liberties.

Whether Winters can truly reverse a decade of Republican entrenchment remains uncertain. The corporate interests that funded Hudson's policies remain powerful. The military-industrial complex, centred on Core Dynamics and its allies, continues to shape Federal policy. And the Thargoid threat provides a permanent justification for security measures that might otherwise face repeal. The Federal Politics arc is not merely a story of electoral competition — it is an examination of how democracy functions when corporate power, military necessity, and technological surveillance converge.
