## Description

The Thargoid Contact arc covers humanity's rediscovery of the Thargoids in the 34th century — the opening phase of the Second Thargoid War. It began with the discovery of organic barnacle structures in the Pleiades Nebula and escalated through hyperdictions, station attacks, and full-scale incursions into the Core Systems. The arc transformed the Thargoids from an obscure legend into an existential threat and drove the formation of Aegis, the first tri-superpower defence organisation in human history.

## Narrative

By the 34th century, the Thargoids had been relegated to urban legend. The First Thargoid War (3125–3151) had been fought in near-total secrecy by the Intergalactic Naval Reserve Arm (INRA), and the subsequent century saw the conflict suppressed, distorted, and finally forgotten. Most citizens believed Thargoids were a myth — until 3302, when independent pilots discovered organic structures in the Pleiades Nebula that produced a strange new material: meta-alloys.

These "barnacles" — so named for their resemblance to marine organisms — were initially treated as a scientific curiosity and an economic opportunity. Meta-alloys proved valuable for repairing technical malfunctions caused by Unknown Artefacts, alien objects that had been appearing in human space since 3301. But as pilots explored deeper into the Pleiades, they encountered something far more disturbing: flower-like vessels of unknown design that could pull ships from hyperspace, scan them, and leave their systems temporarily disabled.

The superpowers struggled to coordinate their response. The Federation and Empire defaulted to military posturing, dispatching warships to the Pleiades. President Zachary Hudson declared that "if the Thargoids want a fight, it's a fight they'll get." The Alliance, under Prime Minister Edmund Mahon, pushed for scientific cooperation and convened the first cross-superpower discussions on the threat. Professor Alba Tesreau of the Achenar Research Council emerged as the leading scientific voice, advocating research over premature aggression.

The escalation was rapid and brutal. In 3303, Thargoid vessels began attacking Federal and Imperial ships outright. Station after station in the Pleiades came under assault, suffering catastrophic damage from caustic weaponry. The formation of Aegis in August 3303 represented an unprecedented act of cooperation, but even this unified effort proved insufficient. The Thargoids demonstrated technology far beyond human capabilities: shutdown fields that disabled entire fleets, caustic missiles that melted hulls, and hyperdiction capabilities that made no ship safe.

By 3304, the conflict had spread beyond the Pleiades into the Core Systems. Thargoid incursions targeted populated systems, forcing mass evacuations and devastating orbital stations. The discovery of abandoned INRA bases in late 3303 — and the subsequent revelation that humanity had previously fought and defeated the Thargoids using a biological weapon called mycoid — complicated the moral landscape. Some, like Princess Aisling Duval, questioned whether the Thargoid threat had been manufactured to justify military spending; others, like Salvation, argued that Aegis's cautious approach was doomed to fail.

The Contact arc established the fundamental parameters of the Second Thargoid War: humanity was fighting a species it barely understood, with technology it could not match, and with political institutions ill-suited to existential crisis. The barnacles and meta-alloys that had seemed so valuable in 3302 were revealed to be markers of Thargoid territory — and humanity had been trespassing.
